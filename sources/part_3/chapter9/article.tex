The first two parts of this thesis built biomedical encoders by pretraining
them on public text. This part puts those encoders to work on a real clinical
task. A clinical study follows each of its patients over time and records what
it learns in a structured form called an electronic case report form, or eCRF:
one line per patient, with a fixed set of fields such as the treatment given or
the date it started. The information needed to fill that form is written in the
patient's own reports, the consultation notes, discharge summaries, and
pathology results produced during care. These reports are longitudinal: they are
written at successive moments as the disease unfolds rather than gathered in a
single note, so a value can appear in one report and be revised in a later one.
The task of this part is to read those reports and fill the matching line of the
form, as \Cref{fig:p3-report-to-ecrf} shows on a single report.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig_report_to_ecrf}

\section{Supervision Without a Reference}

Learning this task in the usual way would need examples of reports paired with
the form a clinician filled from them. No such data is available. No open
dataset pairs a French hospital record with a clinician-filled form, and the
records themselves cannot leave the hospital. The supervision for this task
therefore has to be built rather than collected. We separate the evidence
available to us, as summarized in \Cref{fig:p3-evidence}: synthetic records test
the complete longitudinal task, while human-written corpora test narrower forms
of transfer.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig02_evidence_map}

\section{Two Routes to Supervision}

The available public resources provide two incomplete views of the task.
Biomedical articles contain medical entities, but they do not use the fields of
a new study form. Published patient cases describe clinical trajectories, but
they are not sequences of reports written during care. We therefore construct
two forms of supervision, shown in \Cref{fig:p3-supervision-routes}: broad
open-vocabulary annotations for generalist training, and generated longitudinal
records for adaptation to the eCRF.

Both routes use Qwen3-235B with constrained JSON output. For public text, the
model identifies mentions and describes their types, while for the target task it
first generates the reports and only later assigns strings from them to form
fields. In both cases the resulting annotations are silver rather than human
labels.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig03_supervision_routes}


\subsection{Generalist Annotation of Public Text}
\label{sec:gliner-data}

The generalist corpus teaches the extractor to associate spans with descriptions
of medical types. For each passage, Qwen3-235B proposes entity mentions together
with a natural-language description of each type. We then search for every
proposed mention in the source passage and remove the ones we cannot locate, so
that each retained span is guaranteed to appear in the text. Documents left with
no remaining mention are kept as negative examples.

\subsection{Longitudinal Record Generation}
\label{sec:lymphoma-dossiers}

Published cases summarize a patient trajectory in a single narrative, whereas
the target form is filled from a sequence of dated reports. We start from
PMC-Patients~\citep{zhao_pmcpatients_2023}, a collection of 167,034 English
patient cases, and favour cases
that mention a relapse or a second treatment line. Each selected case is
assigned a reproducible fictitious French identity, hospital, city, and anchor
year.

Generation follows the clinical chronology. We first convert the source case into
a structured French timeline and harmonize its dates, ages, and bounded values.
Reports are then generated one at a time, and after each one we extract the
timeline again from the result as a consistency check. As shown in
\Cref{fig:p3-wavefront}, a report at time $t$ receives only the relevant events
that have already occurred.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig04_wavefront}

The generated reports keep the chronology of the source case but replace its
compact summary with documents written at distinct moments of care.

The resulting corpus contains 2,050 longitudinal records, each with a median of
eight reports and 22,314 characters, and 31\% of them include a second treatment
line. We use 410 records for testing and 100 of the remaining 1,640 records for
validation, which leaves up to 1,540 records for training.


\subsubsection{The Silver Reference}
\label{sec:tapdl}

The generated reports are not yet an extraction reference. Each value must be
linked to one of the 89 form fields and located in the complete record, and this
link also depends on time, since the same treatment may fill a first-line field
in one report and a second-line field after relapse. We therefore label each
report together with its date, document type, and corresponding events.

For each report, the language model returns a field name together with a string
meant to reproduce text from that report, but it predicts neither character
offsets nor a normalized value. We recover the position ourselves: we search for
the returned string, fall back to approximate matching when the literal search
fails, and remap the match onto the complete record. When two fields claim
exactly the same offsets, a final rule keeps a single assignment; partial
overlaps are left in place.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig05_tapdl}

One language-model pass over 16,375 reports yields 237,194 span assignments
across the 89 fields. Each stored value is the text found at its final offsets, but this does not
guarantee that the field assignment is medically correct.


\section{Distributional Comparison with Human Text}
\label{sec:stylometry}

The generated records can be internally consistent while remaining different
from human-written clinical reports. As a proxy for real reports we use
PARHAF~\citep{tannier_parhaf_2026}, a set of fictitious but human-written reports
that contains no hospital data, and we compare it with raw PMC-Patients cases,
our generated records, and published E3C clinical
cases~\citep{magnini_e3c_2021}.

For each source, we sample 1,000 texts and represent them with MedEmbed, the
biomedical encoder introduced in \Cref{chap:architectures}, which maps each text
to a single vector in its description space. We then
compare each distribution with PARHAF using MAUVE~\citep{pillutla_mauve_2021},
Fr\'echet embedding distance (FED), adapted from the Fr\'echet distance used for
generative images~\citep{heusel_fid_2017}, maximum mean discrepancy
(MMD)~\citep{gretton_kernel_2012}, and a classifier two-sample test
(C2ST)~\citep{lopezpaz_c2st_2017}.
The results are reported in \Cref{fig:p3-stylometry}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{plots/part_3/output/fig06_stylometry.pdf}
  \caption{Distributional comparison of three text sources with PARHAF: the
  three sources stay clearly distinguishable from PARHAF, with C2ST near 0.99
  against 0.5 at chance.}
  \label{fig:p3-stylometry}
\end{figure}

These measures point in different directions. For MAUVE a higher value means the
two distributions are closer, while for FED and MMD a value of zero means
identical distributions and a smaller value means closer. C2ST reports the
accuracy of a classifier trying to tell the two sources apart, so 0.5 means
chance-level, indistinguishable distributions, and a higher value means more
separable ones.

The distance measures place raw PMC-Patients closest to PARHAF, followed by the
generated records and E3C, while C2ST gives the reverse order. This disagreement
does not support a single ranking of the sources. More importantly, every C2ST
score lies between
0.985 and 0.991, where 0.5 denotes chance classification. All three sources are
therefore readily distinguishable from PARHAF in this representation space.


\section{Limitations}

Approximately 2.3\% of reports are abandoned during annotation. The train and
test partitions were not checked for shared source articles in PMC-Patients.
Because the same model generates the records and proposes their silver labels,
the reference may favour conventions introduced during generation. The generated
records also remain readily distinguishable from the human-written PARHAF
reports in the representation space of \Cref{sec:stylometry}, so they imitate the
task rather than reproduce the surface of real clinical writing.


\section*{Conclusion}

Public resources support two complementary forms of supervision. Broad
annotations teach an extractor to follow new type descriptions, while generated
records provide a synthetic longitudinal task. Aligning these generated field
assignments back to the record makes a task that has no real filled form
evaluable, so the longitudinal eCRF becomes something a model can be scored on
even though the reference remains silver rather than human. The next chapter asks
whether an extractor conditioned on descriptions can use this supervision while
remaining open to fields it was never trained on.
