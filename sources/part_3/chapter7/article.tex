\section{The Lymphoma eCRF}

The previous chapters extracted entities one span at a time. A clinical study
asks for something larger: a complete form, filled from a patient's whole record,
with every value placed in its correct role along the trajectory. This chapter
moves from local extraction to that full form-filling task and measures how the
models we built perform on it.

We cannot evaluate on a real hospital study, so we build a synthetic one that
imitates it as closely as we can. The task fills a lymphoma electronic case
report form (eCRF) of 89 fields from a longitudinal record. The fields cover ten
sections, including disease history, two treatment lines, imaging, and patient
status. Filling the form requires both locating a value and assigning its role
in the trajectory. In \Cref{fig:p3-task}, the same treatment name fills different
fields before and after relapse.

The full form is larger than this two-field example. \Cref{fig:p3-form-preview}
shows a small extract from three of its sections.

\input{sources/part_3/figures/fig01_form_preview}

The synthetic corpus is split into 1,540 training records, 100 validation
records, and 410 test records. No record is a hospital document. Extractive
systems score local span--description pairs, while generative systems produce
all requested fields jointly.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}

The eCRF keeps one value per record and field: the prediction with the highest
confidence. A wrong value contributes one false positive and one false negative.
PARHAF and the FrACCO NER dataset remain multi-mention tasks, where all predictions are
matched greedily by decreasing confidence. The two regimes are summarized in
\Cref{fig:p3-metrics,tab:p3-metrics}.

We report two eCRF metrics. Span-$F_1$ accepts any non-empty overlap with the
reference, making it permissive about boundaries. Value-$F_1$ requires a Jaccard
similarity of at least 0.5 between normalized token sets. It measures surface
agreement and does not measure clinical equivalence. Main model comparisons use paired
confidence intervals across test records. Each extractive model emits a confidence
with every span. We select its decision threshold on the 100 validation records
and apply it once to the test set, so no reported score uses the test set to tune
the threshold. Generative models return values directly and need no threshold.

\section{Results on the Lymphoma eCRF}

\subsection{Raw Model Outputs}

We compare six extractive systems with four sizes from the same generative model
family. Their training objectives and output formats differ, so this is not a
controlled scaling experiment. The reference here is the silver eCRF of
\Cref{chap:synthetic}, produced by the same model family as the generators, so
the comparison measures agreement with a generated reference rather than clinical
accuracy. Before competition between fields, the strongest
generator is the 4-billion-parameter model at 0.672 value-$F_1$; scaling it
further does not help, as the 9-billion-parameter model reaches only 0.665. The
150-million-parameter MC-bio-gliner reaches 0.634, and its best variant reaches
0.650. The raw gap to the strongest generator is therefore 0.038 for the
published model and 0.022 for the best variant, at 27 times fewer parameters.
The raw gap is modest relative to the difference in model size.

\subsection{Field Competition}

An extractive model scores each span--field pair locally, so the same passage can
be assigned to several fields. Field competition resolves these conflicts before
the form is filled. Strongly overlapping candidates compete, the most confident
assignment is retained, and each field keeps one final value. This rule can
resolve competing assignments, but it cannot repair a clinically wrong span.

Field competition improves all six extractive systems. For the published
MC-bio-gliner, value-$F_1$ rises from 0.634 to 0.640. Applying the same rule to
generators is less natural because
their guided JSON already represents a joint answer. It reduces the
9-billion-parameter model from 0.665 to 0.650, a paired change of $-0.0153$ (95\%
CI $[-0.0176, -0.0129]$), as shown in \Cref{fig:p3-decoding-competition}.

After competition, the strongest generator, Qwen3.5-4B, holds the highest
value-$F_1$ at 0.658, with the task-adapted mixture just behind at 0.657; the two
are not distinguishable. The published MC-bio-gliner reaches 0.640, and scaling
the encoder to 400 million parameters does not raise this score. The generators
keep the advantage on span-$F_1$, up to 0.567 against 0.503 for MC-bio-gliner.

The ranking does not depend on the decoding step. Field competition is the
natural decoder for a local extractor, whereas a generator's guided JSON already
gives one value per field, and the comparison before competition reaches the same
conclusion as the one after it.

\section{Beyond the Aggregate Score}

The aggregate value-$F_1$ hides two things: which kinds of error each system
makes, and how well it ranks its own predictions by confidence. The measures in
this section are computed over every committed prediction,
without the confidence threshold used for the reported value-$F_1$, so they
include the low-confidence tail that the operating point discards. Read this
way, the two systems fail differently. Among its errors, MC-bio-gliner assigns a
correct value to the wrong field 48.9\% of the time, against 38.8\% for the
strongest generator, Qwen3.5-4B; more of the encoder's mistakes are role
confusions, the right string placed in the wrong slot. The generator also ranks
its predictions by confidence more reliably: it reaches a lower risk--coverage
area, 0.207 against 0.239, and a higher precision at 50\% coverage, 0.828 against
0.770 (\Cref{fig:p3-risk-coverage}). The encoder commits to more fields per record and abstains less, which
raises its coverage but leaves more of its confidence budget on wrong slots.

  \caption{Risk--coverage curves on the lymphoma eCRF: Qwen3.5-4B keeps a lower area (0.207 against 0.239) and a higher precision at 50\% coverage.}

MC-bio-gliner's practical advantage is its weight memory. Its 150 million parameters
require less than one gigabyte at common precisions, against roughly 8 GB for the
four-billion-parameter generator it matches, and about 18 GB for nine billion, in
bfloat16. The advantage is in memory alone. It does not carry to speed: parameter
count does not imply lower end-to-end latency, and the encoder is not faster per
record when all 89 descriptions are queried.

The extractive question-answering baseline is weaker still, for a structural
reason. A SQuAD-style head returns one main span for each question, while a longitudinal
record may contain several mentions and surface forms of the same value. Our QA
baseline is trained only on the first mention of each field, which discards this
multiplicity. This mismatch helps explain why the biomedical QA encoder reaches
0.417 value-$F_1$, below the description-conditioned encoders.

The QA scores are also poorly suited to selective prediction. On PARHAF, 72.4\%
of its predictions receive the same confidence. Value-$F_1$ remains defined,
but a risk--coverage analysis is not informative when most examples cannot be
ordered by confidence.

\section{Generalization and Transfer}

Two questions remain about the extractor: how much of its accuracy depends on the
number of generated records, and whether it carries to human-written text. For
the first, a separate series uses a low-density variant of
MC-bio-gliner, trained on a corpus in which 22.5\% of documents have no retained
mention. We adapt it with
10, 50, 100, 500, and 1,540 synthetic records. This is not the joint-supervision
model reported in the main table. Value-$F_1$ rises from 0.182
with 10 records to 0.485 with 50 and 0.659 with the full training set
(\Cref{fig:p3-data-efficiency}). This curve measures the use of generated
supervision; it does not estimate the cost or value of equivalent human
annotation.

The second question is external transfer. The synthetic eCRF measures the
complete task but cannot establish transfer to
human-written text. We therefore evaluate on PARHAF and FrACCO NER
(\Cref{tab:p3-external}). PARHAF contains two biomarker types and only 31 test
documents, so small differences are unstable. FrACCO contains three ICD-O types
and about 34 mentions per document, making it a substantially denser NER task.
The MC-bio-gliner corpus contains only 0.2\% of documents with no retained
mention; retraining it to match the sparser annotation density of these datasets
raises PARHAF transfer only marginally.

dense, flat NER task the biomedical NER baseline keeps the advantage, and
MC-bio-gliner is not the strongest model here. This result narrows the scope of the
synthetic eCRF comparison to the longitudinal form-filling task it was built for.

\section{Limitations}

The main limitation is the silver reference. The same model family generates the
records and produces their annotation, so the reference can reward the conventions
of the generator rather than clinical ground truth. The synthetic split has also
not been checked for PMC source articles shared between training and test. No part
of this evaluation uses real hospital records or human annotation, which remains
the necessary step before any clinical use.

\section*{Conclusion}

This part set out to fill a clinical study form from public foundations alone,
with no hospital data, and its central result is that a small encoder comes close
on a synthetic version of that task. A 150-million-parameter biomedical encoder,
trained only on public and generated text and never shown a real hospital record,
comes within a small margin of a generative model trained on exactly the same
data at 27 times fewer parameters, and a task-adapted variant closes that margin.
Before form-level decoding the published MC-bio-gliner reaches 0.634 value-$F_1$,
within 0.038 of the strongest generator, Qwen3.5-4B; its best variant reaches
0.657, level with Qwen3.5-4B at 0.658 after field competition, a difference the
paired interval cannot distinguish. Scaling the generator to nine billion
parameters does not help.

The size gap is also a deployment gap. Confidentiality and the GDPR keep a
hospital's records off any external API, and the only on-site alternative, a
multi-billion-parameter generator, does not fit the modest hardware of a clinical
service. The encoder does: it runs where the data stays, so comparable accuracy
comes with the only compared model a clinical service could host on site. The
generator keeps an advantage on span-$F_1$, on wrong-field errors, and on
risk--coverage, and the encoder trades this ranking quality for its far smaller
footprint.

The three evaluations delimit where the result holds. MC-bio-gliner wins the
longitudinal form it was built for; on the narrower PARHAF biomarkers it beats
the size-matched baseline but not the generator; and on the dense FrACCO NER the
biomedical baseline keeps the lead. Every part of this evidence is synthetic or
silver, with no real hospital record or human annotation in the task. Matching a
fine-tuned generative model at 27 times fewer parameters, with no clinical data,
is the result of this part. Whether it carries to human-written records is the
next thing to establish, and confirming it on French hospital records is the
necessary step before any clinical use.
