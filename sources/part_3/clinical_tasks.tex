The first two parts built a public biomedical corpus and pretrained encoders on
it. This part asks whether those public foundations can support a real clinical
task without any training on hospital data: reading a patient's free-text
reports and filling the structured form that a clinical study keeps for each
patient. The task is demanding because the same value plays different roles as
the disease unfolds, and because no real filled form is available to learn from.
Deployment sharpens the stakes. A hospital can neither send its records to a
cloud API, which medical confidentiality and the GDPR forbid, nor host a
nine-billion-parameter model on the modest hardware of a clinical service.
Matching the accuracy of a large generator is therefore not the only
requirement: a small encoder that runs on site is the only kind of model whose
footprint fits the hardware of a clinical service, which is the same privacy
constraint that motivated the public corpus and encoders of the first two parts. The next three chapters
build task supervision from public and generated text, design an extractor that
can be asked for new fields at inference time, and compare a small biomedical
encoder with much larger generative models. The result they build toward is that
a 150-million-parameter encoder, trained only on public and generated text and
never shown a real hospital record, nearly matches the accuracy of a generative
model about twenty-seven times its size fine-tuned on the same data.
