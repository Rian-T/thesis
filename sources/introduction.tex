\partepigraph{``The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is
that general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective, and
by a large margin. [\ldots] We should build in only the meta-methods that can find and
capture this arbitrary complexity. [\ldots]''}{Richard S. Sutton, \textit{The Bitter Lesson}}

How can we teach an artificial intelligence to read hospital notes when hospital
notes cannot be used to teach it? The notes themselves would be
the best examples because they contain the words, shortcuts, and clinical habits
the system needs to learn. But they describe real patients, so they stay inside
the hospital, and a system trained on them may have to stay there too. This
thesis asks whether public biomedical text can be used instead.

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Those are the words of a doctor, explaining how he makes a diagnosis. They were set down in 1959 by Robert Ledley and Lee Lusted, in the journal \emph{Science}~\citep{ledley_lusted_1959}, and are reproduced in \Cref{fig:ledley-lusted}. Their claim was simple: a diagnosis is a set of steps, and steps can be written down and even carried out by a machine, with the help of logic and probability. Artificial intelligence was a new idea at the time. A 1956 workshop at Dartmouth College had just given it its name, with broad goals like language and learning. Ledley and Lusted set a medical goal of their own: a computer that could weigh a patient's signs toward a likely disease. Their question is also the question of this thesis: where does the knowledge that drives such a system come from, and can a machine be given enough of it to be useful in care?
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
In 1961, Homer Warner, a cardiologist at the Latter-Day Saints Hospital in Salt Lake City, built one of the first medical diagnosis programs. It applied probability to a patient's signs and estimated which congenital heart defect was the most likely~\citep{warner_1961}. The idea was Bayesian: from a table of past cases the program knew how often each sign went with each disease, and it used those frequencies to rank the candidates for a new patient. Its knowledge was the table of frequencies, and the table came from records of patients seen before. A program of this kind could only know what its records had shown it.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
In 1972, F.~T. de Dombal's group in Leeds applied the same approach to acute
abdominal pain. The system distinguished appendicitis from other causes using
probabilities estimated from previous cases. In a prospective trial on 304
patients, it reached 91.8\% diagnostic accuracy, compared with 79.6\% for the
most senior clinician to see each case~\citep{dedombal_1972}. Both the
probabilities and the evaluation, however, came from Leeds.

The approach was later evaluated in a multicentre trial across eight centres,
with more than 250 participating doctors and 16{,}737 patients. This study
measured the doctors using the system rather than the program alone. Their
initial diagnostic accuracy increased from 45.6\% before its introduction to
65.3\% with it, while unnecessary surgery and missed perforations also became
less frequent~\citep{adams_1986}. The system was useful as a clinical aid, but
the trial did not establish that its performance in Leeds transferred unchanged
to other hospitals. A learning system depends on the data from which its
knowledge was built, so a result measured in one setting may not hold in another.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
In the 1970s, medical AI shifted toward \emph{expert systems}: programs that stored specialist knowledge as explicit rules. MYCIN, written at Stanford by Edward Shortliffe as part of his doctoral work, advised physicians on antibiotics for serious blood and brain infections. Its knowledge was a body of IF--THEN rules, each linking observations such as the site of the infection and the shape and staining of the organism to a conclusion about the likely pathogen and the drug to give~\citep{mycin_1975}. \Cref{fig:mycin-rule} shows one such rule. The original system held about 200 rules, a number the project reports as having grown to almost 500 by 1978~\citep{mycin_book_1984}. Each rule was written and checked by hand with infectious-disease physicians.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Real cases are uncertain, and a rule rarely points to a single answer. To handle this, MYCIN attached a \emph{certainty factor} to its inferences, a number from $-1$ to $+1$ that recorded how strongly the evidence supported a conclusion~\citep{mycin_book_1984}. A certainty factor near $+1$ meant the evidence was strong, a value near $-1$ meant it spoke against the conclusion, and values in between let the program combine several weak signs into one graded judgement. With this calculus MYCIN could reason from partial evidence rather than demand certainty, which is what a physician facing an incomplete picture has to do.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The system was then put to a blind test. Yu and colleagues took ten meningitis cases and collected ten therapy recommendations for each: the therapy actually given, the recommendation produced by MYCIN, and those of a medical student and seven Stanford physicians. Eight outside infectious-disease specialists then rated every recommendation without knowing which came from a person and which from a program~\citep{mycin_eval_1979}. The evaluators judged MYCIN's recommendations acceptable in 65\% of cases. The five faculty specialists in the same test scored between 42.5\% and 62.5\%, a mean of 55.5\%, and the actual therapy given scored 57.5\%. By the stricter test of being rated acceptable by a majority of the eight evaluators, MYCIN scored 70\% against a faculty mean of 44\%. On this narrow evaluation, MYCIN matched or outperformed the specialists. The authors nevertheless identified the small number of cases as a major limitation. They also noted that its acceptance by practising physicians, its effect on care, its cost, and its legal status all ``remain to be answered''~\citep{mycin_eval_1979}. The evaluation established the quality of its recommendations on ten cases, not its readiness for clinical use. MYCIN was never used to treat a patient.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
INTERNIST-1 applied the same rule-based approach to internal medicine. Built at the University of Pittsburgh by the internist Jack Myers and the computer scientist Harry Pople, it aimed to diagnose across the whole specialty. Its knowledge base described 570 diseases and the findings that point to them, entered by hand over years, largely from Myers's own expertise~\citep{internist1_1982,qmr_status_1986}. Tested on nineteen published case records, it diagnosed about as well as the hospital clinicians but worse than the senior discussants, and its own authors concluded that the program was ``not sufficiently reliable for clinical applications''~\citep{internist1_1982}. They listed where it broke. It could not reason about anatomy or about time. It could not build a single explanation for a patient with several diseases at once, so it sometimes blamed findings on the wrong cause. It could not explain its own conclusions, which left a physician with an answer and no way to check it.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
This lack of explanation mattered to physicians. Teach and Shortliffe surveyed physicians about what they would accept from a consultation program, and found that doctors wanted a system that explained its advice and left them in control, and rejected one that simply handed down answers~\citep{teach_shortliffe_1981}. Miller and Masarie later gave the rejected design a name, the ``Greek Oracle'' model, a program that pronounces a verdict to be obeyed~\citep{miller_masarie_1990}. The MYCIN evaluators had pointed to the same problem when they wrote that acceptability remained to be answered. A program could win a blind benchmark and still be unusable, because winning the benchmark was not what physicians were asking for.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
MYCIN and INTERNIST-1 exposed a broader limitation of expert systems. Their
knowledge had to be written and checked one rule or one disease at a time, then
updated as medical knowledge changed. Maintaining such a knowledge base was
costly, and its completeness could never be guaranteed. Expert systems could
therefore perform well on narrow tasks, but their hand-built knowledge was
difficult to scale and keep current.

HELP showed that integration into clinical work also mattered. Warner's group
built it into the hospital information system at Latter-Day Saints Hospital,
where it remained in daily use for years~\citep{help_1975}. It received patient
data through the clinical record and returned its advice within the existing
workflow, rather than waiting for a physician to consult it separately.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The wider field underwent two collapses in public support, later called the AI winters. The first followed the 1973 Lighthill survey, which reported that most workers in the field confessed ``a pronounced feeling of disappointment in what has been achieved'', and blamed the failure to anticipate how fast the number of possibilities grows as a problem gets larger~\citep{lighthill_1973}. The second came in the mid- to late 1980s, after a commercial boom in expert systems and the specialised machines built to run them. Membership in the main AI society rose to a peak in 1987 and then fell back to roughly where it had started, the machine vendors lost their market, and government research budgets were cut~\citep{nilsson_2010}. Medical expert systems left routine care largely untouched. \Cref{fig:ai-health-pubs} tracks the same rise and fall in the biomedical literature: papers on expert systems climb through the 1980s, peak around 1991, and then settle into a long plateau.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Machine learning changed how systems acquired knowledge. Instead of relying on
rules entered one by one, they learned patterns from large sets of examples. By
2016, a neural network trained on 128{,}175 retinal photographs detected diabetic
eye disease about as accurately as ophthalmologists~\citep{gulshan_2016}. A year
later, a network trained on 129{,}450 images matched board-certified
dermatologists at distinguishing skin cancers~\citep{esteva_2017}.
\Cref{fig:ai-health-pubs} shows the corresponding growth of deep-learning
research after 2015.

This approach moved the main constraint from writing rules to collecting suitable
examples. Clinical text is difficult both to obtain for training and to use for
evaluation because patient records cannot circulate freely. This constraint
organises the rest of this thesis.
\end{reviewrewrite}
