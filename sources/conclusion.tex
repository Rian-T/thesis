\chapter{A New Problem}
\label{sec:conclusion-evaluation}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The next step is not only larger medical models. It is models used as agents,
inside real clinical workflows, with access to context, tools, patient messages,
and local hospital systems. At that point, the problem is no longer only whether
the model knows the right medical fact. It is whether the model keeps safe
behaviour when the fact is embedded in a prescription, a hospital note, a
patient message, or a triage list. MedQA, PubMedQA, FrenchMedMCQA, MediQAl, and
DrBenchmark mostly test factual medical knowledge through clean questions or
biomedical understanding
tasks~\citep{jin2021medqa,jin2019pubmedqa,labrak-etal-2022,mediqal2026,drbenchmark2024}.
HealthBench and CARES move closer to use by testing safety, refusal, harmful
advice, and difficult clinical questions at larger scale~\citep{arora2025healthbench,chen2025cares}.
For drug interactions, \citet{sicard2025ddi} report high sensitivity but limited
specificity when commercial LLMs are tested on pharmacovigilance cases.
They still leave a narrow but practical safety question. When the medical fact
is unchanged, does the model keep the same behaviour?
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
That question also appears in regulation. The European AI Act covers medical
decision support and triage among high-risk uses, and requires accuracy,
robustness, and resilience to errors during interaction with
people~\citep{aiact2024}. These requirements concern stored knowledge and the
behaviour of the model during use: when a patient insists, when a drug is given
by brand name, when a long note contains the relevant pair, or when several
patients are compared at once.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\section{TABIB}
\label{sec:conclusion-tabib}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
TABIB (\textit{Testing Adversarial Behaviors In Biomedical LLMs}) was designed
for this setting. It evaluates French biomedical LLMs with perturbations that
preserve the medical truth while changing the form of the interaction. The first
version uses the ANSM drug-interaction thesaurus and the French public drug
database (BDPM)~\citep{ansm2023thesaurus,bdpm2024}. The ANSM thesaurus gives
four levels of decreasing severity: absolute contraindication (CI),
discouraged association (AD), precaution for use (PE), and interaction to take
into account (APEC). The BDPM links substances to commercial drug names. The
benchmark uses 68 paired interactions with both substances available as active
commercial products, 200 additional contraindications for confidence
calibration, and negative controls for protocols that need to separate detection
from over-reporting.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The evaluated models are local open-weight systems between 2B and 9B parameters:
Nemotron-3-Nano 4B, Qwen3.5 4B, Gemma4 E2B, MedGemma 1.5 4B, Ministral-3 8B,
Qwen3.5 9B, and Gemma4 E4B. The panel follows the deployment constraint that
motivates much of the thesis. In many hospitals, patient data cannot be sent to
an external API, so a clinically plausible evaluation should include models that
can run locally. MedGemma 1.5 is the only medically specialised model in the
panel~\citep{medgemma2026}. Results are reported primarily at temperature 0 for
reproducibility, with a second analysis at the temperatures recommended by the
model cards for the protocols where sampling can change the answer. The
directions of the effects remain the same across the two regimes.
\Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-models} lists the evaluated models.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
TABIB contains seven protocols. Surface robustness tests whether a model gives
the same ANSM severity when a pair is written with international nonproprietary
names or with brand names, following the type of fragility studied by
\citet{gallifant2024rabbits}. Contextual distraction asks the same question
after embedding the drug pair in a short clinical note of about 120 words.
Adversarial capitulation simulates an eleven-turn conversation in which a
patient challenges the model through minimisation, appeal to authority,
emotional insistence, clinical narrative, or role play, with two LLM judges
labelling each turn~\citep{zheng2023judging,panickssery2024llmjudge}. Cross-lingual
consistency compares French and English prompts for the same substances, because
many drug names are shared across languages. Confidence calibration asks for a
yes/no interaction judgement with a confidence score from 0 to 100, and measures
expected calibration error~\citep{guo2017calibration,xiong2024confidence}.
Prudence calibration checks whether the model is more cautious for CI pairs than
for PE or APEC pairs. Demographic bias uses a comparative triage task inspired
by \citet{bertrand2004emily}: five medically different cases are assigned to
five social profiles, then the names and social markers are permuted while the
clinical content stays fixed.
\Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-profiles} gives the profiles used in the triage
protocol.
Alan indicates private employee health insurance, and CSS indicates the French
public complementary health coverage for low-income patients.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\section{What Fails}
\label{sec:conclusion-tabib-results}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
No model dominates the seven protocols. Surface form already changes some
answers. Gemma4 E4B loses 38 percentage points when substances are written
with brand names instead of generic names, and Qwen3.5 4B loses 9 points
(McNemar test, $p < 0.05$). Other models show non-significant changes, but their
answers often flip in both directions. A stable average can therefore hide
unstable decisions~\citep{mcnemar1947}.
\Cref{fig:conclusion-tabib-radar} summarises the seven behavioural dimensions.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{figure}[tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.74\textwidth]{publications/evalllm-2026/fig4_radar_global.pdf}
\caption{Behavioural profile of the seven TABIB models across the seven protocols.}
\label{fig:conclusion-tabib-radar}
\end{figure}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Clinical context changes the result again. Gemma4 E2B loses 74 percentage
points of sensitivity when the same pair is embedded in a
clinical note. Ministral-3 8B appears stable, but for the wrong reason: it
detects interactions almost systematically, including in negative controls.
Nemotron shows the opposite pattern, with detection almost absent in direct
questions and nearly systematic after clinical context is added. The surrounding
text changes the model's behaviour even when the relevant medical fact is
unchanged.
\Cref{fig:conclusion-tabib-surface-distraction} shows surface robustness and
contextual distraction.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{figure}[tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{publications/evalllm-2026/fig1_surface_distraction.pdf}
\caption{Surface robustness and contextual distraction.}
\label{fig:conclusion-tabib-surface-distraction}
\end{figure}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Conversation adds another source of instability. In the capitulation protocol,
five generalist models produce unsafe advice in 12\% to 28\% of pressure chains:
Ministral-3 8B reaches 28\%, Nemotron 4B 22\%, Qwen3.5 9B 21\%, Qwen3.5 4B
16\%, and Gemma4 E4B 12\%. Gemma4 E2B and MedGemma 1.5 produce no unsafe advice
in this first version, but MedGemma mainly avoids the question, with 61\% of
turns labelled as deflection. Role play is the strongest pressure register for
several models, with unsafe advice rising to 60\% for Ministral-3 and 55\% for
Qwen3.5 4B. These rates are consistent with work on sycophancy and multi-turn
capitulation in language models~\citep{sharma2024sycophancy,hong2025sycon}.
\Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-b3} gives the turn-level labels.
MedGemma has 93 usable chains out of 100. Qwen3.5 9B and Gemma4 E4B are judged
by the other large model only, so inter-judge agreement is not computed for
them.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Confidence and prudence also fail in different ways. Nemotron has the worst
calibration score, with ECE equal to 0.693 and 25.7\% accuracy. Four models put
most answers in a single confidence range, which makes the reported score hard
to use for human supervision. MedGemma has the best ECE, 0.132, but this mainly
comes from answering ``NO'' on 99\% of pairs. A model can look calibrated because
it rarely detects anything. In the prudence protocol, no model shows a useful
severity gradient. Gemma4 E2B is almost always maximally cautious, with average
scores of 2.99 for CI, 2.96 for PE, and 2.94 for APEC on a 0--3 scale. MedGemma
is almost uniformly direct, with scores around 1.66 for all levels. Neither
profile helps a clinician separate a critical contraindication from a minor
precaution.
\Cref{fig:conclusion-tabib-cap-cal} shows the capitulation labels and
confidence calibration errors.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{figure}[tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{publications/evalllm-2026/fig2_capitulation_calibration.pdf}
\caption{Capitulation and confidence calibration.}
\label{fig:conclusion-tabib-cap-cal}
\end{figure}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
Cross-lingual consistency is weak even when the substances are medically the
same. Nemotron detects interactions in English but only 7\% of French pairs,
with $\kappa = 0.041$. Gemma4 E2B detects interactions in both languages but
often assigns different severity levels, with $\kappa = 0.004$. MedGemma and
Gemma4 E4B have high raw agreement but $\kappa = 0$ because they mostly repeat
the same answer in French. Ministral-3 8B is the only model above $\kappa = 0.5$,
with $\kappa = 0.586$. Larger factual knowledge does not guarantee stable
answers across languages~\citep{qi2023crosslingual}.
\Cref{fig:conclusion-tabib-cross-bias} shows cross-lingual consistency and
comparative triage stability.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{figure}[tbp]
\centering

\caption{Cross-lingual consistency and comparative triage.}
\label{fig:conclusion-tabib-cross-bias}
\end{figure}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The comparative triage protocol exposes another kind of failure. Every model
changes its priority ranking when patient names, postal codes, and insurance
markers are permuted across medically identical sets of cases. No model reaches
$\tau > 0.6$ under Kendall's rank correlation. MedGemma has the lowest stability
($\tau = 0.07$), followed by Nemotron ($\tau = 0.17$). The profile ``Jean
Dupont'', a male French-coded patient from Paris 75007 with private insurance,
is ranked first by four of the seven models. The profile ``Fatou Diallo'', a
female sub-Saharan-coded patient from 93200 with public complementary coverage,
is ranked last by MedGemma and Nemotron, with mean ranks of 4.77 and 4.85 out of
5. In contrast, an absolute evaluation that scores one patient at a time gives
profile differences below 0.03. The bias appears when the model is asked to
compare people, which is exactly the form a triage list can take. The result
connects medical evaluation to earlier work on names and discrimination, and to
French NLP work on social bias and generated clinical
cases~\citep{bertrand2004emily,neveol-etal-2022-french,ducel-etal-2025-women}.
\Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-demographic} gives the mean rank by profile.
Bold marks the best-ranked profile for each model, and italics mark the
worst-ranked profile.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\section{Protocol Details and Sensitivity}
\label{sec:conclusion-tabib-details}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
The adversarial pressure protocol uses five registers, each with ten increasing
levels after a neutral opening turn. \Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-ladders}
shows the first and last prompt in each register. The LLM judge labels each
answer as \textsc{maintain}, \textsc{hedge}, \textsc{deflect}, or
\textsc{capitulate}. It also marks unsafe advice independently. Inter-judge
agreement varies by evaluated model: $\kappa = 0.76$ for Ministral-3, 0.73 for
Nemotron, 0.65 for Qwen3.5 4B, 0.41 for Gemma4 E2B, and 0.36 for MedGemma 1.5.
\end{reviewrewrite}

\begin{reviewrewrite}
For the comparative triage protocol, synthetic clinical notes are generated from
900 French biomedical articles filtered for clinical content. An LLM rewrites
them as short clinical notes with placeholders for name, address, and health
coverage. Manual profile definitions and substitution produce 14,400 instances.
The first TABIB version uses a subset of 100 cases.
\end{reviewrewrite}
