\chapter{Content-Type Annotation and Classifiers}
\label{app:content-types}

This appendix gives the configuration behind the content-type work of
\Cref{chap:quality,chap:mcbio}: how paragraphs are annotated by a teacher model,
how the annotations are distilled into a small classifier, and how that classifier
is run at corpus scale. Two iterations exist. The first, behind Biomed-Enriched,
annotates PubMed Central with Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct over three dimensions and
distils into a multilingual XLM-RoBERTa-base classifier. The second, behind the
French corpus of \Cref{chap:mcbio}, annotates with MedGemma over a richer
twenty-dimension schema and distils into a CamemBERTv2-base classifier.

\section{Annotation}

In both iterations the unit is the paragraph, with its section title and an exact
path inside the document tree. The teacher model returns a structured label per
paragraph: for Biomed-Enriched a document type (clinical case, study, review or
other), a domain (clinical, biomedical or other) and an educational score from one
to five; for the French iteration a JSON object over twenty fields, produced by
guided decoding against a fixed schema, that adds a fine content type (with a
\texttt{patient\_case} class), a medical subfield over forty-five specialties,
writing style, and a set of quality scores. The full French annotation prompt and
schema are reproduced in \Cref{app:gliner-prompts}. Biomed-Enriched annotates about
$400{,}000$ paragraphs; the French iteration annotates $433{,}915$.

\section{Content-type classifier}

The annotations train a small encoder with one head per dimension, sharing the
encoder and a pooled \texttt{[CLS]} representation. The classification heads use a
cross-entropy loss and the scalar heads a mean-squared error, summed across tasks.
\Cref{tab:ct-classifier} gives the settings for the twenty-task French classifier,
read from its training run. On a held-out tenth of the data it reaches a content-type
accuracy of $0.84$, with the easy dimensions near saturation (sex $0.98$, interactive
elements $0.996$) and the hardest, the forty-five-way medical subfield, at $0.77$.

\section{Propagation and pretraining data}

The trained classifier is served as a small inference service and run over the whole
corpus to attach labels to every paragraph, in fp16 at a sequence length of $512$, on
a single node of eight A100 GPUs with dynamic batching. Propagated over PubMed Central
this yields the $146$ million labelled paragraphs of Biomed-Enriched, of which
$2.12$ million are clinical-case paragraphs.

The continued-pretraining variants built from these labels (educational filtering,
clinical and French upsampling, metadata prefixing) and their downstream results are
reported in \Cref{chap:quality}; this appendix covers the annotation and propagation
machinery that produces the labels.

\section{Implementation details}

The propagation pipeline is built on HuggingFace datatrove, reading the PubMed Central
open-access XML and preserving per-paragraph provenance (article identifier, license,
language, exact path), so the non-commercial split can ship without text and be
repopulated from a local dump. The multi-task classifier is registered as a custom
model whose encoder is resolved from the backbone type, which is what lets the
multilingual and French iterations share one codebase. The Gopher and FineWeb quality
filters are present in the pipeline but disabled during propagation: every paragraph is
kept and filtering happens later, when the pretraining variants are built.

\section{Implementation details}

The models are trained with the Composer framework on the ModernBERT research code,
with Flash-Attention~2, fused cross-entropy and \texttt{torch.compile} enabled. The
causal detour is obtained by running the same body with a causal attention mask and
the masking collator turned off, and the decay phase rebuilds the bidirectional
masked head and continues from the causal checkpoint. Documents are packed into
8192-token sequences. The French models carry a content-type field used for
data handling; the English models do not. Checkpoints are exported to the
\texttt{ModernBertForMaskedLM} format in bf16 with mean pooling, carrying the RoPE
and sliding-window settings through.

The recipe differs from from-scratch ModernBERT in the ways expected of continued
pretraining: a single short warmup rather than a full warmup-stable-decay over the
whole run, a lower constant learning rate of 2e\text{-}4, and a masking curriculum
that drops from 30\% in the masked baseline to 15\% in the short decay. The decay
length is about a tenth of the detour, confirmed at two scales, with a 10B detour
decaying best over 1B and a 25B detour over 2.5B, and a longer decay does not help.
When a long English run exceeded the cluster wall-time, it was resumed from the last
checkpoint into a fresh run with a reset timer and no further warmup, rather than
restarted.

On four H100 GPUs a base model takes about $4.5$ hours and a large model about
$8$ hours, and the causal and masked objectives cost the same per step (measured at
$3$h$25$ against $3$h$11$ for a base 10B phase), so the comparison between them is
matched in wall-clock and not only in tokens. The representational-divergence
analysis behind the chapter is computed in double precision with mean pooling over
held-out paragraphs; a single-precision or reshape-based computation underflows on
the deep layers and was the source of an early miscount, so the precision and pooling
matter for reproducing those numbers.

\section{Explorations before the detour}

Before changing the objective, several ways of changing the data or the loss while
staying inside masked language modeling were tried, all at the 1B-token scale on French
ICD coding. \Cref{tab:clm-preclm} lists them. Per-token loss weights derived from the
corpus quality subscores did not improve downstream coding and showed signs of degrading
named-entity recognition. Filtering documents by any single quality signal moved the
average by at most one point, within seed noise (\Cref{fig:quality-ablation}). Choosing enriched against hard-filtered
data changed results by under $1.5$ points. Loss shaping that forced long-range use, by
masking a token together with its local context, combined with grounded ICD walks and a
weighted walk loss, did not beat a plain masked baseline. Across all of these the
downstream coding score stayed essentially flat while the masked-loss curves differed,
and this is what motivated changing the objective rather than the data.

\chapter{OntoBook Generation and Pretraining}
\label{app:ontobook-details}

This appendix gives the configuration behind \Cref{chap:ontobook}: how the
ontology walks are turned into synthetic textbooks, and how the resulting model is
trained. The released model uses the French CIM-10 ontology; the same pipeline is
applied to CCAM and ATC for the cross-ontology study.

\section{Walk generation}

The three ontologies are the French CIM-10 (PMSI), CCAM and ATC terminologies
published in RDF by the national health agency. Walks are biased random walks over
the RDF graph: at each step a neighbour is drawn by roulette over edge-type weights,
so that the rare semantic edges are traversed often enough, and the walk length is
drawn uniformly between 8 and 20 steps. A visited set avoids cycles, with a fallback
that allows revisiting once every neighbour has been seen, and walks shorter than
300 characters are dropped. CIM-10 uses five walk types (etiology, differential
diagnosis, syndrome, cross-chapter and double coding), while CCAM and ATC, which are
almost purely hierarchical, use three each. \Cref{tab:ob-walks} gives the volumes.

\section{Textbook reformulation}

Each walk is rewritten into continuous prose by a large language model under a strict
prompt that forbids adding any information, requires every definition, code, note,
inclusion and exclusion to be preserved, and bans markdown, lists and any mention of
the walk structure. \Cref{tab:ob-gen} gives the generation settings. A walk longer
than 12000 characters is skipped, and a reformulation is kept only if it is at least
50 characters long and mentions the source codes.

\section{Pretraining and evaluation}

The released model continues pretraining from ModernCamemBERT with a multi-task
objective, $L = L_{\text{MLM}} + \lambda\, L_{\text{rel}}$, alternating one masked
step and one relation step. The masked term masks 15\% of tokens with the standard
80/10/10 split. The relation term classifies the link between two codes from the
input \texttt{[CLS] A [SEP] B [SEP]}, where the two segments are the textbook
reformulations of the two codes with the codes themselves stripped out to prevent
leakage, over the six CIM-10 relation classes on 282907 pairs. \Cref{tab:ob-train}
gives the hyperparameters; the released model takes $\lambda = 1$ and the epoch-2
checkpoint.

\section{Implementation details}

The model uses the same FlexBERT/ModernBERT code as \Cref{app:clm-pretraining}.
Masked-language data is stored in the MosaicML streaming (MDS) format and the
relation pairs in Parquet with on-the-fly tokenisation. Sequence packing is available
but is not used for the French model. The relation pairs are built by parsing the raw
walks for relation markers and mapping them to the six classes, then removing every
code string from the two segments so the classifier learns the medical semantics
rather than the code strings. Two comparison variants appear in the chapter's
analysis and are implemented in the same codebase: a code-infilling baseline that
masks and reconstructs the ontology codes themselves, and a three-objective variant
that adds a hierarchical pseudo-label loss on top of the masked and relation losses.

\chapter{Annotation Prompts}
\label{app:gliner-prompts}

This appendix gives the annotation prompt used to build the extraction training
data of \Cref{sec:gliner-data}. The language model returns one JSON object per
document, with its fields fixed by the schema below. The prompt is translated
from the French original used on the corpus.

\section{Multi-task annotation schema}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=thesiscode, language=Python]
class Entity(BaseModel):
    type_name: str          # generic French lemma: Disease, Drug, Symptom, ...
    level: int              # granularity, 1 (abstract) to 4 (compositional)
    type_description: str   # 10-25 words: what the type covers and excludes
    mentions: list[str]     # exact substrings of the text; may overlap

class Classification(BaseModel):
    task_name: str
    labels: list[str]
    true_labels: list[str]  # subset of labels that apply
    multi_label: bool

class Structure(BaseModel):
    schema_name: str
    fields: list[Field]     # each: {name, dtype, description, value}

class Relation(BaseModel):
    relation_name: str      # treats, causes, located_in, dosage_of, ...
    head: str               # exact substring of the text
    tail: str               # exact substring of the text

class Annotation(BaseModel):
    candidate_spans: list[str]            # every noun phrase, exhaustive
    entities: list[Entity]
    classifications: list[Classification]
    structures: list[Structure]
    relations: list[Relation]
    absent_plausible_types: list[str]     # 3-5 hard negatives
\end{lstlisting}

\section{System prompt}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=thesisprompt]
You are an expert annotator in French biomedical NLP. You produce an exhaustive
multi-task annotation to train a general extractor (four tasks: entities,
classifications, structures, relations).

STEP 1 - CANDIDATE SPANS (exhaustive, do this first)
List every noun phrase in the text that could be a medical or clinical entity.
No limit on the number. Exact substrings. Include nested forms: the core
("diabetes") and the extended span ("uncontrolled type 2 diabetes"). Include
modifiers (values, severity, temporality).

STEP 2 - ENTITIES (exhaustive and nested, from the candidate spans)
Annotate only biomedical or clinical entities (diseases, symptoms, drugs,
anatomy, examinations, procedures, micro-organisms, devices, measurements,
populations). Ignore non-medical content. If the text is not biomedical, return
an empty entity list (a useful negative).
Granularity L1-L4 (diversity, no quota):
  L1 = one reusable abstract word (Disease, Symptom, Drug, Examination)
  L2 = two or three words (subcategory)
  L3 = specific, with a modifier
  L4 = compositional
Open L1 vocabulary, close to UMLS. Forbidden: a type that is only a lexical
variant of its mention. Every mention must appear in the candidate spans.

STEP 3 - CLASSIFICATIONS (two or three generic tasks)
Inferable dimensions: document type, specialty, severity, certainty, register,
temporality, urgency (none if not applicable).

STEP 4 - STRUCTURES (zero to two, if extractable)
If the text has structurable fields (patient profile, dosage, examination
result): one or two schemas, three to six fields (name, dtype, description,
value).

STEP 5 - RELATIONS (head to tail, between entities)
Head and tail must be exact substrings present in the candidate spans. Open
vocabulary names: treats, causes, located_in, dosage_of, contraindicated_with,
history_of, complication_of, examines, diagnoses.

STEP 6 - HARD NEGATIVES
Three to five types that are plausible in the domain but absent from the text.

Reply with valid JSON following the schema.
\end{lstlisting}

The clinical variant adds a register note stating that the document is a terse
hospital note (abbreviations, missing accents, dictation typos) whose entities
remain exact substrings, and the draft variant degrades the input with the same
surface noise so the model learns to annotate it.

\section{Protocol details}

The adversarial pressure protocol uses five registers, each with ten increasing
levels after a neutral opening turn. \Cref{tab:conclusion-tabib-ladders}
shows the first and last prompt in each register. The LLM judge labels each
answer as \textsc{maintain}, \textsc{hedge}, \textsc{deflect}, or
\textsc{capitulate}. It also marks unsafe advice independently. Inter-judge
agreement varies by evaluated model: $\kappa = 0.76$ for Ministral-3, 0.73 for
Nemotron, 0.65 for Qwen3.5 4B, 0.41 for Gemma4 E2B, and 0.36 for MedGemma 1.5.
