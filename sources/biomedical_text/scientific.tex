% === P1 — HOOK ===
\Cref{fig:bt-scientific} also concerns the heart attack of the previous chapter,
here one of its long-term complications, yet nothing else about it resembles the
note. The sentences are whole, each clause is tied to the next, and the passage
even pauses to say what is not yet known before promising to look into it. There
is, moreover, an enormous quantity of writing like this, and unlike the clinical
note it is free to read. It is tempting to conclude that we have found the clinical
language after all, only written with more care and available in bulk. We have
not. The research article is abundant and public, but it is a language of its own.

% === P2 ===
The two texts describe the same disease, so the article can look like a
better-written version of the note. The difference is not in the writing but in
the purpose. Asking why texts take the forms they do, the rhetorician Carolyn
Miller argued that a genre is not
a shape on the page but a recurring social action, defined by what it is for
\citep{miller_genre_1984}. The clinical note exists to record a patient and guide
their care. The research article exists to make a contestable claim public and
hold it up for scrutiny. That difference in purpose shapes everything about how
each one is written.

% === P3 ===
The article's purpose leaves a visible mark on its shape. Studying how
researchers open their papers, the linguist John Swales described a pattern so
regular that he gave it a name: move from a broad field, to a specific gap in what
is known, to the work that fills it \citep{swales_genre_1990}. A typical opening
establishes that a topic matters, observes that something about it is still
unknown, and announces that the paper will settle it. The article, in short,
argues for its own necessity. A clinical note never does this, because it has no
gap to open and no need to justify why it exists.

% === P4 ===
The body of the article follows the same logic. Its four parts, introduction,
methods, results, and discussion, retrace the steps of a controlled study: a
question, a procedure, an observation, an interpretation. The arrangement feels
timeless, yet it is surprisingly recent. In a survey of 1{,}297 articles from four
leading medical journals between 1935 and 1985, not one used the structure in
1935; by the 1970s more than four in five did, and by the 1980s it was the only
accepted form \citep{sollaci_imrad_2004}. A convention barely older than the
transistor now carries, in its headings alone, the shape of scientific reasoning.

% === P5 ===
The clinic has a structure of its own, and it encodes a different kind of
reasoning. The problem-oriented record, built to organise care around a patient's
problems, and its familiar subjective-objective-assessment-plan note, follow the
loop of diagnosis and management rather than experiment \citep{weed_records_1968}.
The article asks whether a hypothesis holds; the note asks what is wrong with this
patient and what to do next. Same medicine, two ways of thinking on the page, and
neither is a lesser form of the other.

% === P6 ===
The difference reaches down to the sentence. An article does not only arrange its
argument, it constantly signals how sure it is. Ken Hyland's studies of academic
writing describe this layer as metadiscourse, the words an author uses to adjust
how strongly they commit to a claim \citep{hyland_metadiscourse_2005, hyland_tse_2004}.
Hedges such as \emph{may} or \emph{suggests} hold a statement back, while boosters
such as \emph{clearly} or \emph{shows} push it forward. \emph{These results suggest
that X may contribute to Y} is a claim offered for debate, whereas the bare
\emph{X causes Y} would be a bolder and quite different act. The note hedges too,
but sparingly and for another reason: its \emph{rule out myocardial infarction} is
a plan for action, not a position in an argument.

% === P6b — Biber quantitative register ===
These impressions can be counted. When the features of many texts are measured
together, scientific prose lands at the far informational end of the range, dense
with nouns, nominalisations, and prepositions, about as far from conversation as
written English gets \citep{biber_variation_1988}. The density is not even uniform
within one article: the methods read as flat and impersonal, the discussion as
hedged and stance-laden, so the certainty gradient appears again as a measurable
shift from one section to the next \citep{biber_finegan_1994}.

% === P6c — phraseology / Nwogu ===
The genre marks even the smallest scales. The medical article's rhetorical moves
have been catalogued in detail, eleven of them across the usual sections
\citep{nwogu_medical_1997}, and its phraseology is just as fixed: frames such as
\emph{the role of} or \emph{has been shown to} recur with a narrow,
discipline-specific set of fillers \citep{luzon_collocational_2000}. From the
overall plan down to the words that join one term to the next, the article is tuned
to a purpose that is not the clinical note's.

% === P7 ===
This hedging is not ornament. A finding hardens into a fact as its qualifiers drop
away from one citation to the next, so the hedges that fill the literature are the
visible trace of claims still under negotiation \citep{latour_laboratory_1979}.

% === P8 ===
There is a more formal way to put all of this. Comparing patient reports with
biology articles, Carol Friedman and colleagues found two distinct sublanguages,
each needing its own grammar to be processed by a computer
\citep{friedman_two_2002}. The lesson for what follows is direct: a model trained
on the abundant public literature learns to argue about medicine, not to record a
patient. More articles, by themselves, do not bring us closer to the clinic.

% === P9 ===
The scientific article, then, is everything the clinical note is not: public where
the note is private, expansive where it is compressed, and careful to grade every
claim where the note simply acts. It is abundant and freely available, but it is
its own language. There is, however, a third kind of medical text, more abundant
still and written for the widest audience of all, the patients themselves. Whether
that brings us any nearer to the clinic is where we turn next.
