% === P1 — HOOK ===
\Cref{fig:bt-scientific} is about the same heart attack as the last chapter, this
time one of its later complications. Yet it looks nothing like the note. The
sentences are complete, each one connects to the next, and the passage even pauses
to say what is still unknown and promises to study it. There is also a huge amount
of writing like this, and unlike the clinical note it is free to read. It is
tempting to think this is the clinical language after all, just written with more
care and easy to find. It is not. The research article is abundant and public, but
it is a language of its own.

% === P2 ===
The two texts describe the same disease, so the article can look like a tidier
version of the note. What separates them is their purpose. The rhetorician Carolyn
Miller asked why texts take the forms they do, and argued that a genre is a
recurring social action, defined by what it is for and not by how it looks on the
page \citep{miller_genre_1984}. The clinical note exists to record a patient and
guide their care. The research article exists to make a claim public and open it to
scrutiny. That difference in purpose shapes how each one is written.

% === P3 ===
The article's purpose shows in its shape. The linguist John Swales studied how
researchers open their papers and found a pattern so regular that he named it: move
from a broad field, to a specific gap in what is known, to the work that fills it
\citep{swales_genre_1990}. A typical opening states that a topic matters, points out
that something about it is still unknown, and announces that the paper will settle
it. In short, the article argues that it needs to exist. A clinical note never does
this. It has no gap to open and nothing to justify.

% === P4 ===
The body of the article follows the same logic. Its four parts, introduction,
methods, results, and discussion, retrace the steps of a controlled study: a
question, a procedure, an observation, an interpretation. The arrangement feels
timeless, but it is recent. In a survey of 1{,}297 articles from four leading
medical journals between 1935 and 1985, not one used this structure in 1935; by the
1970s more than four in five did, and by the 1980s it was the only accepted form
\citep{sollaci_imrad_2004}. A format only a few decades old now carries the shape of
scientific reasoning in its headings alone.

% === P5 ===
The clinic has a structure of its own, and it encodes a different kind of reasoning.
The problem-oriented record was built to organise care around a patient's problems,
and its familiar subjective-objective-assessment-plan note follows the loop of
diagnosis and management rather than experiment \citep{weed_records_1968}. The
article asks whether a hypothesis holds. The note asks what is wrong with the
patient and what to do next. Both are about the same medicine, but they reason in
different ways, and one is not a worse version of the other.

% === P6 ===
The difference reaches down to the sentence. An article does more than arrange its
argument. It keeps signalling how sure it is. Ken Hyland's studies of academic
writing call this layer metadiscourse, the words an author uses to adjust how
strongly they commit to a claim \citep{hyland_metadiscourse_2005, hyland_tse_2004}.
Hedges such as \emph{may} or \emph{suggests} hold a statement back, while boosters
such as \emph{clearly} or \emph{shows} push it forward. \emph{These results suggest
that X may contribute to Y} is a claim offered for debate, while the plain \emph{X
causes Y} would be much bolder. The note hedges too, but rarely and for another
reason: its \emph{rule out myocardial infarction} is a plan for action, not a
position in an argument.

% === P6b — Biber quantitative register ===
These impressions can be counted. Measured across many texts, scientific prose is
about as far from everyday conversation as written English gets, full of nouns,
long words, and prepositions \citep{biber_variation_1988}. It is not even uniform
within one article. The methods read flat and impersonal, the discussion hedged and
personal, so the same shift in certainty appears as a measurable difference between
sections \citep{biber_finegan_1994}.

% === P6c — phraseology / Nwogu ===
The pattern reaches the smallest scale. The medical article's rhetorical moves have
been catalogued in detail, eleven of them across the usual sections
\citep{nwogu_medical_1997}, and its set phrases are just as fixed: frames such as
\emph{the role of} or \emph{has been shown to} recur with a narrow,
discipline-specific set of fillers \citep{luzon_collocational_2000}. From its
overall plan down to its smallest phrases, the article is built for a purpose the
clinical note does not share.

% === P7 ===
This hedging does real work. A finding hardens into a fact as its qualifiers drop
away from one citation to the next, so the hedges that fill the literature are the
visible trace of claims still being argued over \citep{latour_laboratory_1979}.

% === P8 ===
There is a more formal way to say all of this. Carol Friedman and colleagues
compared patient reports with biology articles and found two distinct sublanguages,
each needing its own grammar to be processed by a computer
\citep{friedman_two_2002}. The lesson for what follows is plain. A model trained on
the abundant public literature learns to argue about medicine, not to record a
patient. More articles, on their own, do not bring us closer to the clinic.

% === P9 ===
So the scientific article is very different from the clinical note. It is public
where the note is private, spells things out where the note leaves them unsaid, and
weighs each claim where the note simply acts. It is abundant and free to read, but
it is its own language. There is, however, a third kind of medical text, more
abundant still and written for the widest audience of all, the patients themselves.
Whether that brings us any nearer to the clinic is where we turn next.
