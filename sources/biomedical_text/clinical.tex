% === P1 — HOOK ===
\Cref{fig:bt-clinical-note} is a discharge summary, written by one cardiologist
for another. It is also, to a reader of ordinary French, barely a text at all.
Articles and verbs have dropped out, findings arrive as fragments, drugs are
named by brand and dose, and the diagnosis is a code. Most of it would look, to
an outsider, like careless writing: abbreviations without explanation, units
fused to numbers, sentences that never finish. The natural reaction is to call
it bad French and to clean it up. We argue the opposite. The note is not
degraded general French but a different system, with its own grammar, written
for readers who already know almost everything it leaves out.

% === P2 ===
This intuition has a precise name. Studying how specialised communities write,
the linguist Zellig Harris observed in the 1960s that the language of a technical
field is not the general language with a smaller vocabulary, but a system in its
own right \citep{harris_mathematical_1968}. He called it a \emph{sublanguage}: the
sentences a community produces about a narrow domain, held together by rules about
which words may go together that are tighter than, and sometimes absent from, the
wider language. Those rules are what matters, because in a sublanguage only
certain classes of words combine, so its grammar sits partly outside ordinary
usage rather than inside it as a subset. Harris and his colleagues later made this
concrete, reconstructing the grammar of a small immunology literature from its
articles alone and showing that the field's knowledge was, in effect, carried by
its sublanguage \citep{harris_immunology_1989}.

% === P3 ===
The clinical note is the best-documented case of this idea. One of the earliest
attempts to make computers read medical records, Naomi Sager's Linguistic String
Project at New York University, set out to turn free-text clinical reports into
structured data, and in the process found that the notes already followed a
grammar of their own \citep{sager_nlip_1981, sager_mlp_1987}. To read
them, the program needed rules for the short, verbless phrases that notes use but
everyday language does not, together with categories taken from medicine itself,
such as patient, finding, and treatment. A line like \emph{afebrile, chest clear}
is not broken language; it follows rules, just not the ones taught at school.
Those rules hold for a plain reason: a community of clinicians keeps writing this
way, and newcomers are trained to do the same \citep{kittredge_sublanguages_1982}.
The grammar lives inside the hospital, among the people who share it.

% === P4 ===
Seen up close, the strangeness of the note is not carelessness but compression.
Sager and colleagues showed that clinical shorthand follows a small set of
deletion rules: the writer drops a subject here, a verb there, a tense elsewhere,
but only when the reader can put it back \citep{anderson_compression_1975}.
\emph{Chest films normal} stands for \emph{the chest films were normal};
\emph{no bone pain} stands for \emph{the patient has no bone pain}. What
disappears is the part the reader least needs spelled out. The note is short on
purpose, and short by rule.

% === P5 ===
This works because the reader is an expert. Writing for a colleague, the
clinician leaves out whatever that colleague can already supply, following the
ordinary logic of cooperative communication: say what is needed, and no more
\citep{grice_logic_1975, sperber_relevance_1995}. The same words behave
differently depending on who reads them. \emph{Afebrile, vitals stable, continue
antibiotics} is complete for a physician and almost empty for anyone else. The
note is not written to stand on its own; it is written to be finished by a reader
who already knows the rest.

% === P6 ===
That shared background has a name. The note records only the update to a large
body of assumptions the writer and reader already hold in common: medical
training, the patient's history, the routines of the ward
\citep{stalnaker_common_2002}. None of it appears on the page. This is worth
pausing on, because the compression that makes the note efficient is the same
thing that makes it opaque from outside. A model trained on public text is not
merely missing a few words; it is missing the shared ground that gives the words
their meaning.

% === P7 ===
The note does more than describe the patient; much of it acts. The plan gives
orders and makes commitments, and a few entries change the world simply by being
written, such as \emph{DNR}, \emph{discharge}, or \emph{transfer to intensive
care} \citep{austin_words_1962, searle_speech_1969}. Written by an authorised
clinician, the three letters \emph{DNR} do not report a fact so much as create
one. A record that
carries acts of this weight is signed, audited, and legally binding, which is one
more reason it is kept under lock.

% === P8 ===
Even the shape of the note has a history. Its habit of pinning each finding to a
site in the body, of reading the patient as a map of localised lesions, descends
from the clinical gaze that took form with the modern hospital
\citep{foucault_clinique_1963}.

% === P9 ===
There is a blunter reason the note stays inside the hospital: it is saturated with
personal data. A single report can carry the patient's name, age, and dates, a
home address, a family situation, and a diagnosis rare enough to identify someone
on its own. Stripping the obvious identifiers is not enough, since a pseudonymised
note is still not anonymous, and a model trained on such text can memorise and
later surface what it read \citep{tannier_parhaf_2026}. Confidentiality here is
not a rule added on top of the text; it is part of what the text is made of.

% === P10 ===
For French, this scarcity is sharper still. There is no shareable corpus of real
French hospital records to match the large English ones, and there has not been
one for a decade \citep{neveol_clinical_2018}. What is public stands in for the
real thing: published case reports, drug package leaflets, article abstracts. A
case report, though, is written to teach, and it reads as a small narrative in
full sentences and past tense, as in this opening from a French clinical-case
corpus: \emph{``B.A., âgé de 36 ans, sans antécédents notables, a été admis en
février 1994 pour des douleurs lombaires droites''} \citep{grabar_cas_2020}. Set
beside the clipped admission lines of \Cref{fig:bt-clinical-note}, the change of
register is immediate.

Whether such proxies can stand for true hospital notes is openly disputed, and
the disagreement runs deep. The authors of that case-report corpus argue the two
are very close, since the same physicians write both \citep{grabar_cas_2020}; a
more recent corpus of expert-written reports holds the opposite, that the style
of case reports is in fact quite different from that of electronic health records
\citep{tannier_parhaf_2026}. The field cannot yet agree on whether the text it is
allowed to share even resembles the text it cannot. The text we most want to
model is the text we can least obtain.

% === P11 ===
Taken together, these properties explain why the clinical note is so hard to
obtain. It is rare because it is private, and private because of what it is: a
closed sublanguage, written in compressed form for an expert in-group, dense with
personal data, and performing acts that carry legal weight. Its scarcity is not an
accident of collection that more effort would remove; it follows from the nature
of the text itself.

% === P12 ===
And yet medicine produces text in vast quantities. Most of it is not the clinical
note at all, but the published literature, abundant and free to read. This raises
the question the next chapter takes up: can that abundance stand in for the
language of the clinic?
