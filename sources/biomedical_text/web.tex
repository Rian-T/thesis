% === P1 — HOOK ===
\Cref{fig:bt-web} shows the same heart attack one more time, now in the words of
the people who go through it. There is no technical vocabulary here. A worried
relative writes \emph{crise cardiaque}, not \emph{infarctus du myocarde}, and
describes the symptoms in plain, everyday words. This kind of text is the most
common medical writing of all. There is far more health information written for
patients than there are journal articles, and far more than there are clinical
notes. It is clearly not a clinical record. But it is worth a look, because it
shows what happens to medical language when it reaches the public, and above all
what happens to names.

% === P2 — Fleck ===
The idea that a fact changes as it travels is an old one. In 1935 the physician
Ludwik Fleck argued that knowledge is made inside a community of specialists and
then changes as it moves out to a wider audience \citep{fleck_genesis_1979}. The
cautious \emph{it appears that} of the laboratory becomes the public's
\emph{doctors have shown}, and the careful technical term becomes an everyday
word. Writing for patients is one more layer of the same knowledge, with its own
form.

% === P2b — the drug leaflet: official, mandated vulgarisation ===
Not all of this lay writing is unofficial. The most systematic patient-facing
text of all is the drug leaflet, the \emph{notice} folded inside every box of
medicine. In the European Union it is not optional. By law every approved drug
carries one, in the official language of each market, written ``in clear and
understandable terms for the users'' \citep{ec_directive_2001_83}. A shared
template sets the same six headings everywhere, from \emph{what the medicine is
and what it is used for} to \emph{possible side effects}, and a draft must be
tested on real patients for readability before it is approved
\citep{ema_qrd_template}. Here the move from specialist to public that Fleck
described is not left to chance. It is written down and checked. The leaflet also
shows the variation in names directly, because the rules require it to print the
brand name next to the common one, \emph{Doliprane} next to \emph{paracétamol},
so that one substance carries its several names side by side on a single page.

% === P3 — naming varies (Wüster foil, Cabré, Gaudin) ===
The thing that changes most, for our purpose, is the naming. The founding goal of
terminology, set out by the engineer Eugen Wüster, was one concept and one term,
each thing with a single correct name \citep{wuster_einfuhrung_1979}. Real usage
does not work this way. The linguist María Teresa Cabré showed that one concept
is named in different ways depending on who is speaking and why, and that this
variation is normal \citep{cabre_theories_2003}. In French, Gaudin's
socioterminology makes the same point about how terms spread from specialists to
the public \citep{gaudin_socioterminologie_2003}. The heart attack of the
clinical note, \emph{infarctus du myocarde}, coded \emph{I21}, is the same event
the patient calls a \emph{crise cardiaque}. One concept, several names, and most
of them come from the public.

% === P3b — Temmerman: concepts are fuzzy too ===
The problem goes deeper than names. Rita Temmerman showed that many medical
categories have no sharp edges. They group around typical cases, they shift over
time, and they are often understood through metaphor, the heart as a pump that can
fail \citep{temmerman_towards_2000}. So normalisation faces two problems at once.
One concept carries many names, and the concept itself has soft boundaries.

% === P4 — UMLS, measured ===
Medicine has not solved this. It has only catalogued it. The Unified Medical
Language System, described by Bodenreider, brings more than sixty vocabularies
into one map that links names to concepts \citep{bodenreider_unified_2004}. The
map is many-to-many. In its 2004 release it held about 2.5 million names for some
900{,}000 concepts. It does not pick one name per concept. It records them all.
The dream of one concept, one term did not come true, and our tools simply store
every name instead.

% === P4b — ontology vs running text ===
This is why a formal ontology does not just replace the text. An ontology is built
to state what exists, in clean and consistent terms \citep{smith_ceusters_2010}.
A clinical note states what a clinician believes at one moment, with its hedges,
its timeline, and everything left unsaid. The two go together, but they are not
the same. The UMLS can list every name for a heart attack, but it cannot record
that this patient's was \emph{probable}, \emph{three days ago}, and now
\emph{resolved}. Linking the running text to the codes means translating between
the two, which is harder than looking a word up in a table.

% === P5 — Hacking + register, brief ===
Naming in medicine is not neutral. As Ian Hacking observed, classifying people
can change the people classified, so a label can reshape the very thing it names
\citep{hacking_social_1999}. And the registers sit measurably apart. The language
of patients leans on pronouns and everyday words, while clinical and scientific
writing pile up nouns and exact terms \citep{biber_variation_1988}.

% === P6 — synthesis + bridge (closes the part) ===
The three texts complete the picture. The same medicine is written in three ways.
They differ in how much there is, from the rare clinical note to the abundant
article to the even more abundant page for patients. They differ in form, in
reasoning, and most of all in how they name things. The text we can gather most
easily is the furthest from the text we really need. So using public data for the
clinic is not only a question of quantity. It means crossing from one register to
another, and turning the many names of a thing into the one the clinic uses. That
is the problem the rest of this thesis takes up.
