% === P1 — HOOK ===
\Cref{fig:bt-web} shows the same heart attack once more, but this time in the
words of the people who live through it. There is no technical vocabulary anymore. A
worried relative writes \emph{crise cardiaque} (\emph{heart attack}) rather than
\emph{infarctus du myocarde} (\emph{myocardial infarction}). The symptoms are
described in plain, everyday words. For at least two decades the internet has made
this kind of text widely available. By 2013, 72\% of US internet users had looked online for health
information \citep{fox_health_online_2013}, creating a vast and freely accessible
body of patient-facing medical text. This text is far from the
language of the clinical domain, but it offers something research articles do not: an
abundant and more varied representation of medical knowledge. It is therefore worth
examining how medical language, and especially medical terms, change when they reach
the public. 

% === P2 — Fleck ===
The idea that a fact changes as it travels is an old one. In 1935 the physician
Ludwik Fleck argued that knowledge is first made inside a small circle of
specialists, and is then changed as it moves out to a wider audience
\citep{fleck_genesis_1979}. As knowledge reaches the public, cautious claims become
more direct and technical terms are replaced by everyday words. Writing for patients
therefore presents the same knowledge in a different form, adapted to a different
audience.

% === P2b — the drug leaflet: official, mandated vulgarisation ===
Not all of this lay writing is unofficial. The most systematic patient-facing text
of all is the drug leaflet, the \emph{notice} folded inside every box of medicine.
In the European Union, by law, every approved drug must carry one. It must be in the
official language of each market where it is sold, and written ``in clear and
understandable terms for the users'' \citep{ec_directive_2001_83}. A shared
template fixes the same six headings everywhere, from \emph{what the medicine is
and what it is used for} to \emph{possible side effects}. A draft must also be
tested on real patients for readability before it can be approved
\citep{ema_qrd_template}. This makes the move from specialist to public explicit.
The leaflet also shows how names vary. The rules require the brand name to
sit beside the common one, \emph{Doliprane} next to \emph{paracétamol}. So a
single substance carries several of its names side by side on one page.

% === P3 — naming varies (Wüster foil, Cabré, Gaudin) ===
What changes most, for our purpose, is the naming. When terminology was founded as
a field, the Austrian engineer Eugen Wüster set its goal as one concept and one
term. Each thing should have a single correct name, so that technical communication
could never be misread \citep{wuster_einfuhrung_1979}. Real usage does not work
this way. The linguist María Teresa Cabré showed that a single concept is named
differently depending on who is speaking and to whom. This variation is
a normal property of terms
\citep{cabre_theories_2003}. In French, François Gaudin's socioterminology makes
the same point from a social angle. It follows how terms travel from specialists to
the public and collect new names along the way
\citep{gaudin_socioterminologie_2003}. The heart attack of the clinical note,
\emph{infarctus du myocarde}, coded \emph{I21}, is the very same event the patient
calls a \emph{crise cardiaque}. One concept, many names, and most of them are the
public's.

% === P3b — Temmerman: concepts are fuzzy too ===
The trouble runs deeper than the names. The terminologist Rita Temmerman showed
that many medical categories have no sharp edges at all. They form around typical
cases rather than strict definitions. They shift over time. They are often
grasped through metaphor, the heart understood as a pump that can fail
\citep{temmerman_towards_2000}. Normalisation therefore faces two problems at
once. One concept is carried by many names, and the concept it points to is itself
blurred at the boundary.

% === P4 — UMLS, measured ===
Medicine has not solved this. It has only catalogued it. The Unified Medical
Language System, described by Olivier Bodenreider, gathers more than sixty
vocabularies into a single map that links names to concepts
\citep{bodenreider_unified_2004}. It clusters the synonyms of one idea under a single
concept, so one concept can carry many names. In its 2004
release it held about 2.5 million names for some 900{,}000 concepts. That is about three
names for every concept on average. It keeps them all. Wüster's dream of one concept
and one term never came true, and the field's main tool simply stores every name
instead.

% === P4b — ontology vs running text ===
This is also why a formal ontology cannot just stand in for the text. An ontology
is built to state what exists in the world, in clean and consistent terms
\citep{smith_ceusters_2010}. A clinical note states only what one clinician
believes at one moment, with its hedges, its timeline, and everything it leaves
unsaid. The two work together, but one cannot replace the other. The UMLS can
list every name for a heart attack. It cannot record that this patient's attack
was \emph{probable}, happened \emph{three days ago}, and is now \emph{resolved}.
Tying the running text to the codes means translating between these two. That is
far harder than looking a word up in a table.

% === P5 — Hacking + register, brief ===
Naming in medicine is not neutral. As the philosopher Ian Hacking
observed, classifying people can change the people classified. A label
feeds back on and reshapes the very thing it names \citep{hacking_social_1999}.
The registers also sit measurably apart. As Douglas Biber's work on text variation
shows, the language of patients leans on pronouns and everyday words, while clinical
and scientific writing pile up nouns and precise terms \citep{biber_variation_1988}.

% === P6 — synthesis + bridge (closes the part) ===
The three texts complete the picture. The same medicine is written three ways. They
differ in how much of each there is, from the rare clinical note to the abundant
article to the still more abundant page written for patients. They differ in form,
in reasoning, and most of all in how they name things. The text we can gather most
easily is the furthest from the text we actually need. Using public data for the
clinic is therefore more than a question of quantity. It means crossing from one
register into another. It also means turning the many names of a thing back into the
single one the clinic uses. That is the problem the rest of this thesis takes up.
